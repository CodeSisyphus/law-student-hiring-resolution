A resolution on accelerated law-firm recruiting, sponsored by the Bar Association of the District of Columbia.

What we ask the House to adopt

A resolution urging law schools, legal employers, bar associations, and other stakeholders to:

  1. Study how accelerating recruitment timelines affect the quality of legal education, the well-being of law students, and the long-term health of the entry-level hiring market; and
  2. Consider, in their own recruiting practices and policies, the educational, professional, and equitable interests of law students.

It mandates nothing. It blames no one. It asks the profession to study a problem it has already begun to name.

Why it matters

Since 2024, hiring for the largest sectors of private practice has moved into the first semester of the first year, and sometimes the first weeks. Students now make career-defining choices before they have learned to read a case, take a cold call, or sit for a single exam. A majority of the Class of 2028 report that this timing harmed them, and three in four surveyed students tie rising anxiety directly to recruiting pressure. The change also narrows access: students with family or industry connections navigate the compressed calendar far more easily than first-generation students, who report the lowest awareness of these timelines before they enroll. This is not students against firms. Giving the first year room to breathe means better-prepared students, better-matched hires, and classrooms that can teach again.

"It should give us pause that one of the forces exerting the most pressure on the structure of the first-year curriculum is not pedagogical reform or accreditation standards, but employer recruiting activity." Nikia Gray, Executive Director, National Association for Law Placement (NALP)

Four concerns, one modest response

1. Integrity of the first-year curriculum

The 1L year builds a lawyer's analytical, ethical, and professional foundation. When recruiting begins in week one, employer activity, not pedagogy, drives the calendar.

2. Student well-being

Students face professionally consequential decisions before their first exam. Seventy-five percent of surveyed students attribute increased anxiety specifically to recruiting pressure.

3. Equity and access

Only 25% of students knew these timelines before enrolling, and that awareness concentrated among continuing-generation graduates and the most selective schools.

4. Health of the hiring market

Employers now hire on less information about performance and interest. Commentators call it a "lose-lose-lose" that no single party can solve alone.

The students asked first

On January 1, 2026, student governments at 18 law schools (Columbia, Cornell, Duke, Georgetown, Harvard, Michigan, Northwestern, NYU, Penn, Stanford, Texas, UC Berkeley, UCLA, Chicago, Virginia, Vanderbilt, WashU, and Yale) jointly asked the ABA to review accelerating recruitment. Several backed the request with supermajority student polls.

Why the ABA

No single firm can step back without losing talent to the firm beside it, and no single school can either. Only a national body can prompt the collective action no one player can start alone, and that is the ABA's role. By urging study, it also produces what the profession still lacks: reliable, shared data on what this calendar is doing to legal education, and the footing to adopt a measure adequate to the problem. The resolution prescribes no timeline and assigns no blame. It orders the diagnosis, not the cure.
