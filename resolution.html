A Resolution Before the ABA House of Delegates Annual Meeting · Chicago · 2026
Law Student Hiring Cycle Resolution Sponsored by the Bar Association of the District of Columbia, with a coalition of law schools

The Resolution, in full

Accelerated law-firm recruiting and the first year of legal education.

Resolution

RESOLVED, That the American Bar Association urges law schools, private legal employers, bar associations, and other stakeholders in the legal community to study the impact of accelerating law firm recruitment timelines on the quality of legal education, the well-being of law students, and the long-term health of the entry-level legal hiring market; and

FURTHER RESOLVED, That the American Bar Association urges law schools, private legal employers, bar associations, and other stakeholders in the legal community to consider, in their respective recruitment practices and policies, the educational, professional, and equitable interests of law students, including their need for adequate time to develop the analytical, ethical, and professional competencies that the first year of legal education is designed to foster.

The report

The recruitment of law students for post-graduate employment has long been anchored by the on-campus interview (OCI), traditionally held in late summer between a student's first and second year. That predictability let students focus on the academic and professional development the first year is designed to provide. Since 2024, the timeline for recruiting into the largest sectors of private practice has accelerated sharply. This resolution responds by urging the legal community to study the impact and to consider, in its own practices, the interests of law students.

I. The acceleration of law-firm recruiting

NALP's Perspectives on 2025 Law Student Recruiting, released in January 2026, tracked the 2025-26 cycle. A majority of offers for 2L summer positions were extended before June of the first year, and 85% before July, a single-year jump from 34% the prior cycle. Five percent of summer-associate offers were made in March of the 1L year or earlier, 15% in April, and 36% in May. During the 2025 cycle for 2026 summer programs, 80% of offers came through employer-sponsored recruiting and only 20% through traditional school-sponsored methods such as OCI.

For the first time, NALP tracked "jumbo offers," extended to a student for both 1L and 2L summer positions at once. Ten percent of offices with 1L summer programs reported making at least one, and 87% of those tied a bonus or financial incentive to the 2L portion. The offer rate to 1L summer associates reached an all-time high of 94.2%, while the acceptance rate for those return offers fell to 72.3%. The offer-acceptance rate for post-graduation associate positions exceeded 89% for the fourth straight year, reaching 89.4% in 2025.

"It should give us pause that, in a period already defined by significant institutional change and disruption, one of the forces exerting the most pressure on the structure of the first-year curriculum is not pedagogical reform or accreditation standards, but employer recruiting activity." Nikia Gray, Executive Director, NALP (January 2026)

A June 2026 LSAC-NALP report was the first to focus on how accelerated recruiting affects the first-year experience. It found that 88% of 1Ls surveyed in October 2025 were aware of Big Law timelines for 2L summer positions, but only 25% had been aware before starting law school. Higher pre-matriculation awareness concentrated among men, continuing-generation college graduates, and students at the most selective schools. More than half of the Class of 2028 polled reported that accelerated recruitment negatively affected them.

II. Concerns across the legal community

First, the integrity of the first-year curriculum. The 1L year is where practitioners build the analytical, ethical, and professional competencies that carry through a career. When recruitment begins in the first semester, and sometimes the first weeks, students make consequential choices before developing the competencies that should inform them. As Lois Casaleggi, Associate Dean for Career Services at the University of Chicago Law School, observed: "The academic disruption this is causing is not sustainable. . . . We need law students to become law students first." Professor Jamie Abrams has written that the environment "harms legal education pedagogy, creates a logistics nightmare for students and educators, [and] imposes psychological harms on students."

Second, the well-being of law students. A substantial body of research documents elevated anxiety and depression among law students. Industry analysis of the 2025-26 cycle links the accelerated timeline to negative mental-health impact, with 75% of surveyed students attributing increased anxiety specifically to recruiting pressure. One 1L told the LSAC-NALP study: "It's been extremely overwhelming for my mental health, and I feel like I'm drowning most of the time." Accelerated recruitment also narrows opportunity: with both summers decided early, students lose the chance to explore different practice areas, and public-sector paths that hire later become harder to preserve. Post-graduation public-sector placements fell 14% from 2024 to 2025 and federal clerkships fell 11%, though part of that reflects a graduating class roughly 7% smaller.

Third, equity and access. Accelerated timelines privilege students with pre-existing connections. First-generation college graduates, Pell Grant recipients, and part-time students reported significantly lower pre-matriculation awareness. As legal-education scholars put it, "super early recruiting . . . surely exacerbates disadvantage and privileges advantage in ways that make the game harder than it needs to be for all sorts of students, including many first-generation law students." At the institutional level, compressed hiring favors the most prestigious schools and closes before students can demonstrate their talent.

Fourth, the health of the legal hiring market. Employers, too, now decide on less information about academic performance, professional development, and demonstrated interest. Commentators describe a "lose-lose-lose situation" in which students, firms, and schools all bear costs none can address alone. Marcie Davis, Assistant Dean of Career Services at SMU Dedman School of Law, has called it "a feeding frenzy that no one seems to know how to stop."

III. The coalition of law-student governments

On January 1, 2026, student governments at eighteen law schools submitted a joint letter to the Council of the ABA Section of Legal Education and Admissions to the Bar, requesting review of accelerating recruitment timelines. The letter detailed four categories of concern: undermined first-year educational quality; premature narrowing of career choice; disparate impact on students without pre-existing access to legal-industry networks; and structural effects on law-school staff and employer recruiting teams. Several signatories attached student polls showing supermajority support. After dialogue with ABA leadership, the coalition was encouraged to pursue a resolution before the House of Delegates and reached out to the Bar Association of the District of Columbia, which, on the recommendation of its Young Lawyers Section, agreed to sponsor it.

IV. The appropriate role of the ABA

As the national voice of the profession, the ABA can convene law schools, legal employers, bar associations, and students in a coordinated examination that no other institution can. Its relationships with the federally recognized law-school accreditor, entities such as the Law Student Division and Young Lawyers Division, and state and local bars make it the natural forum. The resolution is intentionally general: it directs no particular reform, endorses no timeline, and assigns no blame. It urges study and consideration while the data is still new, recognizing that any durable solution must emerge from collaboration rather than a single directive.

V. Conclusion

The data, the testimony of deans and career-services professionals, the analyses of legal employers, and the voices of affected students all point the same way: the current environment is not sustainable, and its consequences extend well beyond the students struggling to navigate it. The ABA should adopt the resolution and take its proper place in a conversation the legal community has already begun.

Relevant existing ABA policy

The resolution builds on, and does not disturb, existing policy: 23A523 (urging legal employers to evaluate law students holistically and consider more than grades and class rank) and 24A515 (promoting pay transparency during the on-campus interview process).
